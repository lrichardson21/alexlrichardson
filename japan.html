Ibaraki Christian University

I know I didn't peak in high school, but I may have peaked at IC (a joke). But seriously, this was more than an internship to me. It changed the way I view culture and relationships. In terms of my journey with learning Japanese, this was an invaluable experience. It catalyzed my understanding of the language by answering a key question: how does culture, how does actually being in the country where this language is spoken shape one's understanding of it? So as soon as I arrived I began absorbing as much as I could. I was constantly taking notes: writing down everything from food label ingredients to street signs. As I made friends and chatted with strangers I created a dictionary of all the new words I was hearing, which I eventually turned into flashcards.

Despite all of this intake and the studying I had been so committed to for months prior, nothing could have prepared me for the impact of being thrust into speaking in real live conversations. Embarrassing! At first I was so frustrated by my inability to speak with any amount of fluency. Even though I could understand probably around 50% of what was being said at this point, I had a really hard time replying, because this was a muscle I wasn't able to flex in the US. But over the course of the following three months i was able to drastically improve my speaking skills and confident - which I am incredibly grateful for. I often think about all the strangers I met in bars, restaurants, shops who put up with my broken Japanese and struggled through conversations with me, offering any and all guidance they could and sometimes even resorting to charades to communicate with each other. By the end of my time in Japan, I was able to have a 3 hour long conversation with non-English speaking friends, the same group of friends I had to use google translate with on the first day of my internship.
